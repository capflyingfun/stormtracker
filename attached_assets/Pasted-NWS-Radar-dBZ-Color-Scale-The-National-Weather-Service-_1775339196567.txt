NWS Radar dBZ Color Scale
The National Weather Service (NWS) uses a standardized reflectivity color table to display radar data in dBZ (decibels relative to Z), which measures the strength of the radar echo from precipitation. Higher dBZ values indicate stronger returns, often from heavier rain, hail, or other hydrometeors Github.

Standard NWS Color Mapping (Base Reflectivity) Github

Interpretation by dBZ Github+1
0–20 dBZ: Light to moderate rain; blues and greens.

20–35 dBZ: Moderate to heavy rain; yellows, oranges, reds.

35–50 dBZ: Heavy rain and possible hail; dark reds, magenta, violet.

50+ dBZ: Extreme rain, hail, or debris; white/bright white.

Rain rate approximations (hourly) National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration+1:

20 dBZ ≈ light rain (0.02 in/hr)

30 dBZ ≈ 0.10 in/hr

40 dBZ ≈ 0.45 in/hr

50 dBZ ≈ 1.90 in/hr

60 dBZ ≈ 8.00 in/hr

65 dBZ ≈ 16+ in/hr (large hail possible)

Key Notes
The NWS has published multiple standardized color tables over time; the above is the current common scheme.

Colors are designed for quick visual interpretation of precipitation intensity.

For severe weather, look for bright white (≥60 dBZ) and magenta/violet (50–60 dBZ) as indicators of hail or extreme rain.

If you want to explore variations or download the exact NWS color table for your radar app, resources like WxTools list official NWS color tables for different radar sites www.wxtools.org.
