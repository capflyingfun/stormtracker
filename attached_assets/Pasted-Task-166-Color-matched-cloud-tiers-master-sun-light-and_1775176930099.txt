Task #166 - Color-matched cloud tiers, master sun light, and day/night cycle

What & Why

The 3D view currently shows all clouds in their baked model colors which
makes it hard to visually distinguish storm intensity at a glance. Clouds
should be tinted to match the dBZ color tier (blue, green, yellow, orange,
red, magenta) so you can instantly see severity. Adding a master sun
directional light gives dramatic shadows and depth. A day/night mode based
on actual sunrise/sunset times makes the scene feel alive and grounded in
reality.

Done looks like

- Each cloud tier has a color tint matching its dBZ intensity color
- Blue tier clouds have a cool blue-white tint
- Green tier clouds have a green-white tint
- Yellow tier clouds have a warm yellow tint
- Orange tier clouds have a dark orange tint
- Red tier clouds have a deep red-dark tint
- Magenta tier clouds have a dark purple-magenta tint
- A directional sun light casts dramatic light across the scene
- Sun position and intensity changes based on actual time of day
- Nighttime has moonlight with dark blue ambient
- Daytime has warm sunlight with light blue ambient
- Sunrise/sunset have golden orange light at low angle
- Scene background color shifts to match time of day
- Fog/atmosphere color matches time of day for depth
- All changes respect existing glow PointLight system
- Performance stays smooth on mobile

Tasks

1. Color-tint clouds per tier

In makeCloudGroup3D() and in loadCloudModels3D() where materials are
assigned, tint the cloud color based on the dBZ tier. Do NOT use
MeshStandardMaterial (performance issue from Task 164). Instead, modify
the MeshBasicMaterial color property to blend the baked model color with
the tier color.

Define tier tint colors:
  blue (0-30 dBZ):     blend toward #88ccff (cool blue-white)
  green (31-40 dBZ):   blend toward #44ff88 (green-white)
  yellow (41-45 dBZ):  blend toward #ffee44 (warm yellow)
  orange (46-51 dBZ):  blend toward #ff8800 (dark orange)
  red (52-60 dBZ):     blend toward #ff2222 (deep red)
  magenta (60+ dBZ):   blend toward #cc00ff (dark purple-magenta)

Use a lerp blend of about 40-60% tint mixed with the original baked color
so the model texture still shows through but the tier color is obvious.

Apply the tint when cloning the model in makeCloudGroup3D():
  child.material = child.material.clone()
  child.material.color.lerp(new THREE.Color(tierTintHex), 0.5)

Also apply to the fallback procedural sphere clouds so they match.

2. Add master directional sun light

Add a THREE.DirectionalLight to the scene that represents the sun or moon.
Since we are using MeshBasicMaterial for clouds (which ignores directional
lights), this light will primarily affect any MeshStandardMaterial or
MeshPhongMaterial objects in the scene like the terrain if it uses those.

BUT more importantly, use the sun position to control:
  - Scene background color (sky color)
  - Fog color and density
  - Ambient light color and intensity
  - The color temperature of the glow PointLights

This gives the VISUAL EFFECT of sun lighting without needing expensive
materials on the clouds.

3. Implement day/night cycle based on real time

Use the sunrise and sunset times that are already available from the
Open-Meteo forecast data (S.forecast.daily.sunrise and S.forecast.daily.sunset).

Calculate sun elevation angle:
  - Get current time, sunrise time, and sunset time
  - Calculate sun progress through the day as a 0-1 value
  - Convert to elevation angle (0 at horizon, 90 at noon, negative at night)

Based on sun elevation angle, set these scene properties:

  NIGHT (elevation below -6 degrees):
    Sky/background: #050810 (very dark blue-black)
    Fog color: #050810
    Ambient light: #112244 at intensity 0.15
    Glow lights: full intensity (storms glow bright against dark sky)

  TWILIGHT (elevation -6 to 0 degrees):
    Sky/background: blend from #050810 to #1a1a3a (deep blue-purple)
    Fog color: match sky
    Ambient light: #334466 at intensity 0.25
    Glow lights: 80% intensity

  GOLDEN HOUR (elevation 0 to 10 degrees):
    Sky/background: blend from #1a1a3a to #ff8844 near horizon fading
      to #4488cc overhead
    Fog color: #cc7744 (warm golden haze)
    Ambient light: #ffaa66 at intensity 0.4
    Glow lights: 60% intensity (ambient light partially competes)

  DAYTIME (elevation above 10 degrees):
    Sky/background: #1a3355 (medium dark blue, not bright since this is
      a storm tracker and dark theme looks better)
    Fog color: #2a4466
    Ambient light: #88aacc at intensity 0.5
    Glow lights: 40% intensity (daylight reduces glow drama)

  Tip: Keep the overall scene darker than a realistic daytime sky since
  the app has a dark theme and bright daylight would clash. Think
  "overcast storm day" not "sunny afternoon".

4. Add sun/moon visual indicator

Add a small bright sphere or sprite in the sky at the calculated sun
position to give a visual reference point. During day use a warm yellow
sphere with a soft glow. At night use a smaller cool white sphere for
the moon. During twilight fade between them.

Position the sun/moon on a large radius arc based on the elevation angle
and place it in the correct compass direction (east in morning, south at
noon for northern hemisphere, west in evening).

5. Update the scene on refresh

When storm data refreshes or when the user switches to the 3D tab,
recalculate the sun position and update all the day/night properties.
Also run the calculation once per minute on a timer so the scene
gradually shifts if someone leaves the 3D view open.

6. Add a manual override toggle (optional but nice)

In the 3D view controls near the Filter/Reset/Glow buttons, add a
small toggle that cycles through: Auto (real time), Day, Night, Golden.
Default is Auto. This lets users force a specific lighting mood.
Store the preference in localStorage as st_3dLighting.

Notes

- Do NOT switch cloud materials to MeshStandardMaterial. Keep
  MeshBasicMaterial for clouds per Task 164. The day/night effect comes
  from sky color, fog, ambient light, and glow intensity modulation.
- The color tinting on clouds uses material.color.lerp which works
  perfectly with MeshBasicMaterial.
- Keep mobile performance in mind. The sun position calculation is cheap.
  Fog is cheap. Changing background color is cheap. No expensive shader
  changes needed.
- The _tierMaterials cache in makeCloudGroup3D needs to store tinted
  materials per tier, not share untinted ones.
- Test with 100+ zones on mobile to verify performance is still smooth.
