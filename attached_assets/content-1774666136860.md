## Storm Movement Prediction

This calculator determines the approximate direction of a storm’s movement relative to the surface based on upper troposphere wind speed and the Coriolis parameter.

## Explanation

**Geostrophic Approximation:** In the Northern Hemisphere, the Coriolis force deflects moving objects to the right. A westward-moving storm in the upper troposphere experiences a southward deflection due to the Coriolis force. The magnitude of this deflection depends on the wind speed and the Coriolis parameter. This simplified calculation assumes a geostrophic balance, where the pressure gradient force is balanced by the Coriolis force. A more complete analysis would require considering other factors like friction and ageostrophic winds.

## Related Questions

**Q: Why does the Coriolis effect cause a southward deflection in this case?**

**A:** In the Northern Hemisphere, the Coriolis force deflects moving objects to the right. Since the storm is moving westward, the Coriolis force acts southward, causing the storm to appear to move slightly southward relative to the surface when observed from a geostationary satellite.

**Q: Are there limitations to this simplified calculation?**

**A:** Yes, this calculation is a simplification based on the geostrophic wind approximation. It neglects other forces that can influence storm movement, such as friction near the surface and ageostrophic winds. Therefore, it provides only an approximate direction of storm movement.
